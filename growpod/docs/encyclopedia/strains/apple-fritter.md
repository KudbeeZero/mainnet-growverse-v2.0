# Apple Fritter

Apple Fritter is a potent, sweet-and-pastry-forward hybrid created by Lumpy's Flowers (Jason Dias) in Northern California's Napa wine region. A cross of Sour Apple and Animal Cookies, it is the brand's most iconic and best-selling cultivar, prized for its dessert-like apple-and-butter flavor and a powerful, balanced high that leans gently indica.

Cannabinoid testing typically places THC in the low-to-high 20s, commonly cited around 24% with phenotypes reaching up to 31%, and CBD is negligible (under 1%). Effects open with a burst of euphoria, mental clarity and giggly, social energy before settling into a deeply relaxing, tingly body high that stops short of full sedation, making it a versatile late-afternoon-to-evening strain.

The flavor and aroma are its calling card: sweet baked apple and buttery pastry over an earthy, faintly diesel-and-cheese backbone, driven by a caryophyllene-dominant terpene profile rounded out by myrcene, limonene, pinene, humulene and linalool.

## Lineage & history

Apple Fritter was bred around 2013 by Jason Dias, co-owner of Lumpy's Flowers, a self-described craft cannabis legacy brand based in Northern California's Napa region. It is a cross of Sour Apple (Sour Diesel x Cinderella 99, originally from Top Shelf Seeds) and Animal Cookies (GSC x Fire OG, part of the legendary Girl Scout Cookies line). The strain rose to fame after winning 2nd Place in the Hybrids category at the 2016 NorCal High Times Cannabis Cup, and the original elite clone ("Lumpy's Cut") remains the gold-standard phenotype. Because the prized cut is largely clone-only, seed-grown versions from various seedbanks show notable phenotype variation.

## Growing notes

Apple Fritter grows as a bushy, stocky, indica-leaning plant with tight, dense, resinous bud structure. It flowers in roughly 8-10 weeks indoors, with outdoor harvests around early-to-mid October. Indoor yields are moderate, around 450-500 g/m2, with outdoor plants producing up to 500-600 g each. The dense buds make it sensitive to humidity and bud rot, so it rewards low-humidity environments, good airflow, defoliation and plenty of light; this finickiness plus the need to source a good cut make it moderately difficult, better suited to growers with some experience.
