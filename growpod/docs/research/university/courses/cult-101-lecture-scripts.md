## Lesson `orientation-intro` — Orientation (~3 min spoken)

*"Welcome back. If you've come from Foundations of Plant Biology, you now understand the plant as an
organism — the cell, the energy economy, the pump. This course is where you put your hands on it. I'm
Professor Flora, and over the next stretch we're going to take a seed and walk it all the way to a jar.*

*Three ideas organize everything. First: a plant lives at the mercy of two kinds of forces — the
abiotic ones, the non-living dials you control like light, temperature, water, and air, and the biotic
ones, the living things that want a piece of your plant, the pests and molds. A great grower is just
someone who keeps the abiotic dials in range and the biotic threats out. Second: the plant moves
through a cycle — germination, seedling, vegetative, flower, harvest — and each stage wants different
things from you. Give a flowering plant what a seedling needs and you'll stall it. Third, and this is
what separates a grower from a gardener: you write things down. The grower who logs height, water, and
climate can see the future — they can forecast the harvest — because the plant is astonishingly
consistent when you are.*

*Your final exam isn't a quiz. It's a harvest. To finish this course, you'll bring one real plant all
the way to harvest in your grow. Let's make sure you can. First, a quick ungraded check of where you're
starting."*

## Lesson `m1-narrated-lesson` — Module 1: Abiotic & Biotic Factors (~11 min)

*"Let's name the dials, because once you can see them you stop guessing. The abiotic factors are the
non-living inputs, and there are really just a handful that matter. Light — both how much and how long;
the plant counts daylength to decide whether to grow or to flower, which is a lever you'll pull on
purpose later. Temperature — too cold and everything slows, too hot and the plant shuts its stomata to
survive, and you'll remember from biology that a closed stomata means the pump is off. Water — too
little and it wilts, too much and you drown the roots and invite rot; more new growers kill plants with
kindness, with overwatering, than with neglect. Air — fresh air carries the carbon dioxide the plant
builds sugar from, and moving air keeps the leaf surface dry and the stems strong. And humidity, which
you'll learn to read together with temperature as a single number later in the program.*

*Here's the unifying idea, and it's the one from biology again: Liebig's barrel. The plant grows only
as well as its most limiting factor allows. Perfect light means nothing if the roots are starved for
air. So your job is never to maximize one dial — it's to find the one that's holding everything back and
fix that. Walk the dials in order and the worst one announces itself.*

*Now the biotic factors — the living threats. Pests like spider mites and fungus gnats; diseases like
powdery mildew and bud rot. The lesson here is mindset: you don't react to an infestation, you deny it a
home. Most biotic problems are really abiotic failures wearing a costume — mildew loves the stale, humid,
cool air you'd get from poor airflow; gnats love chronically wet soil. Fix the environment and you've
prevented most of the disease. We call it integrated pest management, and its first principle is
prevention through environment. So when you see a pest, ask the grower's question: what condition did I
let happen that invited this? In the lab, you'll steer a few of these dials and watch the plant — and its
uninvited guests — respond."*

## Lesson `m2-narrated-lesson` — Module 2: The Seed-to-Harvest Cycle (~12 min)

*"A cannabis plant doesn't grow at one speed in one mode — it moves through chapters, and each chapter
asks you for something different. Let's walk them.*

*Germination: the seed cracks and sends down a root. It needs warmth and moisture and almost nothing
else — no nutrients, no strong light. Overcomplicate this stage and you rot the seed. Then the seedling:
the first true leaves appear, and the plant is fragile and small. Gentle light, light watering, high
care, low intervention. Its root system is tiny, so it drinks little; this is exactly where overwatering
strikes.*

*Then the engine turns on — vegetative growth. Given long days, the plant builds structure: stem,
branches, fan leaves, roots. This is when it stretches and fills out, when it wants strong light, real
food, and room. Everything you build here is the frame that will hold flowers later; a weak veg makes a
weak harvest. You stay in veg as long as you want a bigger frame.*

*Now the decision that defines indoor growing: you shorten the days. When the plant senses long nights,
it reads winter coming and flips to flower. It stretches once more — sometimes doubling in height, a
thing you must plan your space around — and then it stops making structure and starts making buds. The
pistils emerge white; over the weeks the flowers fatten, the trichomes you studied in biology shift
clear to cloudy to amber. The plant is now spending everything it banked. Its needs change — it wants
less nitrogen, a careful climate, and a clean, airy canopy so all that dense flower doesn't rot.*

*Finally, harvest — and harvest is a judgment, not a date. You read the plant: mostly cloudy trichomes
with a little amber is the classic window for peak potency. Cut too early and you leave potency and
weight on the table; too late and it slides toward a heavier, sleepier profile. Then it's dried slowly,
then cured in jars — and curing is not optional aging, it's where harsh smoke becomes smooth and the
aroma blooms. The plant taught you its whole life in one cycle; do it once and the second time you'll
see every chapter coming. In the sim you'll scrub through a full cycle and call each stage by its needs."*

## Lesson `m3-narrated-lesson` — Module 3: Growing Practices & Data Collection (~11 min)

*"Knowledge without habits is just trivia, so this module is about what you actually *do* — and what you
*write down*. Start with watering, the skill new growers most often get wrong. The rule isn't a schedule;
it's the plant and the pot. Water thoroughly, then wait until the medium is genuinely light and dry-ish
before watering again — that wet-then-dry swing pulls air down to the roots, and roots need air as much
as water. Lift the pot; learn its full and empty weight in your hand. You're not watering on Tuesdays,
you're watering when the plant needs it.*

*Feeding: less is more, especially early. The plant tells you when it's hungry, and thanks to biology you
can read it — mobile-nutrient hunger shows low and old, immobile shows new and high. Feed to the stage:
more nitrogen in veg for green growth, a shift away from it in flower. And start light; it is far easier
to add food than to flush out too much.*

*Training: simple, cheap interventions shape the plant. Remember apical dominance from biology — top the
plant and you trade one tall cola for several even ones; bend a branch down and the plant promotes it.
You're not decorating, you're redistributing the plant's own growth hormones to build a flat, even canopy
that uses your light.*

*Now the habit that turns all of this into foresight: data. Every visit, log a few numbers — height, what
you watered, the climate, anything you noticed. Three things happen. You catch problems while they're
small, because a trend is visible long before a crisis. You learn your specific plant and room, which no
generic guide can teach you. And you can forecast — a plant's height and stage and history predict its
harvest window and its yield with real accuracy, because the plant is consistent when the grower is. The
log is how you stop reacting and start anticipating. Your capstone is a real harvest; the growers who log
get there cleanly. In the lab you'll keep a short grow journal and use it to call the harvest."*
